---

### SC2 — Planning ("Sold As Is") — Most Important Special Condition

This is the most significant clause in the contract. In full effect, it means:

- The vendor gives **no warranty** that improvements comply with any Building Legislation
- You **cannot claim compensation** for building legislation non-compliance
- You **cannot call on the vendor to bear costs** of complying with Building Legislation
- Settlement is **not conditional** on you obtaining any consent or approval
- You **cannot object, claim compensation, or delay payment** due to:
  - Any matter in this special condition
  - **Any loss, damage, dilapidation, infestation, defect (latent or patent), or mechanical breakdown**
  - The presence of any contaminant
  - The condition or existence of services

**"Latent or patent defects" means hidden AND visible defects.** By signing, you waive your right to claim against the vendor for anything the B&P finds — whether it was discoverable before signing or not.

**Practical implication:** This makes pre-signing B&P non-negotiable. If you sign first and B&P then reveals waterproofing failure under the new bathroom tiles, structural concerns about the renovation, or asbestos in the original construction, you have no recourse against the vendor. You're stuck. The only exit point after signing is during the 3-business-day cooling-off period (at a penalty of $100 or 0.2% of price, whichever is greater). B&P typically takes 3–5 business days, so sign-first-then-B&P under cooling-off is extremely tight. **Do B&P before signing.**

---

### SC3 — Purchaser's Inspection

By signing, you warrant that:
- You are satisfied with the nature, quality, condition, and state of repair
- You accept the property **subject to all defects (latent or patent) and all dilapidation and infestation**
- You have not relied on any information, representation, or warranty from the vendor

This reinforces SC2. Together, SC2 and SC3 create the strongest "sold as is" position in either contract reviewed.

### Deposit

The Particulars of Sale template states: **"Deposit = 10% of the Price on the signing hereof."**

At $870k: 10% = **$87,000 deposit**  
At $840k: 10% = **$84,000 deposit**

With ~$92,700 available cash, a 10% deposit would leave essentially nothing for stamp duty (~$47,000) and other costs. **This is impossible without additional funds.**

You must negotiate the deposit to **5%** when submitting your offer. This is common for FHBG buyers. Gadens will likely accept this as it is standard practice. The blank deposit field in the contract is filled at the time of offer — insert your negotiated 5% figure explicitly.

At $870k with 5% deposit: deposit = $43,500. Cash position:
- Deposit: $43,500
- Stamp duty: ~$47,700
- Conveyancer + B&P: ~$2,000
- **Total: ~$93,200** — exceeds your $92,700 by ~$500. Essentially zero buffer.

At $840k with 5% deposit:
- Deposit: $42,000
- Stamp duty: ~$44,400
- Other: ~$2,000
- **Total: ~$88,400** — leaves ~$4,300 buffer from $92,700. Workable.
