## 5. Covenant K411388 — The Title Encumbrance

**This is the most distinctive legal finding in this contract.** The covenant K411388, registered 09/06/1983, is a positive covenant imposed by the Urban Land Authority when Lot 91 was originally sold to the Yap family in 1983 for $11,000.

**The covenant requires:**
> The transferees covenant with Urban Land Authority pursuant to Section 14 of the Urban Land Authority Act 1979 that they will within a period of five years following the date hereof erect or cause to be erected on the said land a house for their own occupation. This covenant shall forever run at law.

**Current status:** The obligation was to build a house within 5 years of June 1983 (i.e., by June 1988). A house clearly exists — the obligation was fulfilled over 35 years ago. The Urban Land Authority no longer exists (it was dissolved and succeeded by various bodies, ultimately Housing Victoria / Homes Victoria).

**Why it still shows on title:** The covenant was registered as a permanent encumbrance. It has not been formally removed from title, even though the positive obligation was satisfied long ago.

**Practical implications for a new owner:**
1. The building obligation is spent — you are not required to do anything further under this covenant.
2. The Urban Land Authority Act 1979 has been repealed. The covenant's enforcing body no longer exists.
3. Under Victorian law, positive covenants (obligations to DO something) generally do not run with the land to bind subsequent purchasers in the same way restrictive covenants do. The Yaps covenanted to build — that was their personal obligation, satisfied when they built the house.

**Action required:** Have your conveyancer formally confirm in writing that K411388 imposes no ongoing obligations on a new purchaser, and that the covenant is effectively spent. This is a routine confirmation for Victorian conveyancers — Urban Land Authority covenants from the 1977-1985 era are common in Belmont/Highton/Fyansford suburbs. If there is any doubt, a letter from Homes Victoria (the successor body) confirming the covenant is spent can be obtained.

**Risk level:** Low-moderate. Almost certainly a historical artifact with no ongoing force, but it must be formally cleared by your conveyancer before signing.

## 6. Building Permits: "Not Applicable" — Is This Correct?

**The Section 32 marks building permits in the preceding 7 years as "Not Applicable."**

**Why this needs scrutiny:** The current vendors bought in January 2023 and have owned for ~3.4 years. During the inspection on 16 May 2026, the following renovation works were observed and attributed to the current owners:
- New kitchen (outstanding quality)
- New main bathroom
- New flooring (throughout)
- New carpets
- New split systems in all 3 bedrooms and main living area

These works occurred after January 2023 — all within the 7-year building permit disclosure window.

**Whether permits were required depends on scope:**

| Work | Permit typically required? |
|---|---|
| New kitchen (cosmetic — cabinets, benchtop, splashback, appliances) | Generally not, if no structural work or plumbing re-routing |
| New main bathroom (wet area waterproofing, shower recess) | Permit required if new shower recess or wet area constructed |
| New flooring/carpets | Not required |
| Split systems (room air conditioning) | Not required |

The bathroom renovation is the most likely trigger for a permit. If the vendor renovated a bathroom with a new shower recess (and therefore new waterproofing) without a building permit, this is an unpermitted works issue — though less severe than Cambra's scope.

**Unlike Cambra, there is no attached owner-builder defect report contradicting the disclosure.** The "Not Applicable" claim is uncontradicted by any attached document. But your B&P inspector should specifically review the renovation works and form a view on whether permits should have been obtained.

**Ask your conveyancer** to formally request confirmation from the vendor about which works were done during their ownership and whether any permits were obtained. If permits exist, they should be produced before exchange.

**What you need to do before signing:**

The pool is also an ongoing maintenance cost: chlorination, pumps, filters, cleaning. Budget approximately $1,000–3,000/year depending on whether it is a self-service or serviced pool.
